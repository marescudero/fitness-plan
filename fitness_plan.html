The honest rule of thumb for weight

You should complete all reps with good form, feeling challenged on reps 8–10, but never feeling your form break down. If you're swinging, shrugging, or holding your breath to finish a rep — the weight is too heavy.

Why 15 lbs is too heavy for some upper body moves

The shoulders, biceps, and triceps are small muscle groups that are very commonly undertrained. Pressing 15 lbs overhead with no training history risks compromising form and straining the shoulder joint. Use the 10 lb kettlebell for these in Phase 1. By Phase 2 you'll likely be ready for 15 lbs.

The 10 lb kettlebell — where it fits

  • Goblet squats — its best exercise, hold by the horns at chest height
  • Upper body isolation work (curls, shoulder press, triceps) — more appropriate starting weight than 15 lbs
  • Kettlebell deadlift — great intro to hip-hinge before RDLs
  • Kettlebell swings — added in Phase 3, one of the best full-body moves you can do at home

What to track

Keep a simple note on your phone after each session — how many reps, and how it felt on a scale of 1–10. When a workout consistently feels like a 4–5 out of 10 in effort, you're ready to add reps, reduce rest time, or move to the next phase.

Schedule notes

  • All sessions fit outside 9–5 — mornings, evenings, or weekends
  • Phase 1: Mon / Wed / Fri — never two days back to back
  • Phase 2 adds Saturday yoga as active recovery
  • Phase 3 adds Tuesday — the only weekday addition
  • Sunday is always full rest, every phase
